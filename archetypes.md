# The Grammar of Failure

## Seven Empirically Derived Archetypes of Investment Thesis Failure

Frequencies derived from 60,000+ Clarity Scores™ benchmarked through 
the RUNE Protocol (patent-pending). Corpus compiled in less than four 
months, representing 12+ months ahead of original projection trajectory.

The fundamental insight from this corpus is counterintuitive: 
**failure is rarely born from bad data. It is born from faulty logic 
applied to good data.**

## Detailed Definitions

### I. The Service Trap

A founder positions a labor-intensive services business as a software 
platform, claiming SaaS-style multiples on revenue that scales linearly 
with headcount. Pattern: revenue grows, but gross margins do not 
expand and operational complexity grows faster than throughput.

**Diagnostic question:** *Does the next dollar of revenue require a 
proportional dollar of cost?*

### II. The Hardware Denial Curve

A founder pitches a hardware-intensive business with capital 
requirements modeled at software-stage scale. Manufacturing 
capitalization is typically under-modeled by an order of magnitude. 
Mass-production milestones are stated with sub-threshold capital asks.

**Diagnostic question:** *Is the capital ask sized for the bill of 
materials at scale, or only for the prototype?*

### III. Super-App Indigestion

A founder launches three or more distinct business lines simultaneously 
at pre-seed, positioning complexity as a moat. Each business line 
typically requires its own go-to-market motion, talent stack, and 
unit economics — none of which are achievable on a pre-seed budget.

**Diagnostic question:** *Which single business line, removed, would 
break the entire thesis?*

### IV. The Structural Kill Shot

A category of signals that render the thesis structurally unfundable 
regardless of execution: unlicensed securities activity, fabricated 
pipeline, commingled customer custody, or guaranteed return mechanics. 
These are not optimization problems. They are categorical defects.

**Diagnostic question:** *Is there any execution path that fixes this, 
or does the model itself need to change?*

### V. The Dangerous Asset Class

The most pernicious failure mode in venture capital: a high Presentation 
Score concealing a low Clarity Score™. Charismatic founders, polished 
decks, narrative momentum — all working to obscure structural defects 
in the underlying unit economics. This archetype produces the largest 
write-downs in the corpus.

**Diagnostic question:** *If I had to defend this investment to a 
hostile LP committee using only the financials, could I?*

### VI. The Regulatory Arbitrage Illusion

A founder builds a business model predicated on exploiting a regulatory 
vacuum. The model works in the short term, but the vacuum is closing — 
and typically closes before the company achieves regulatory-resilient 
scale. The thesis dies not from execution but from inevitability.

**Diagnostic question:** *Is the regulatory exposure asymmetric — and 
does the company have a credible path to regulatory legitimacy at 
scale?*

### VII. The Paradigm Shift

The rarest signal in the corpus. Genuine structural novelty — a thesis 
that does not fit the existing taxonomy of failure modes because it 
operates on different mechanics. The only verdict warranting immediate 
escalation. Detected in fewer than 2% of evaluated submissions.

**Diagnostic question:** *Why does this not fit any of the previous 
six archetypes?*
